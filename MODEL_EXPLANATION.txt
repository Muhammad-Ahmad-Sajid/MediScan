================================================================================
                    BONE FRACTURE DETECTION MODEL EXPLANATION
                     Why We Selected and Designed Our Pipeline
================================================================================

This document details the machine learning models utilized in this project, 
explaining the architectural choices, training strategies, and the technical and 
clinical rationale behind them.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. THE CORE BACKBONE: ResNet-50
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
At the heart of the system is ResNet-50 (Residual Network with 50 layers), a 
deep convolutional neural network pretrained on the ImageNet dataset.

Why ResNet-50?
  * Residual Connections (Skip Connections):
    - Deep networks suffer from the "vanishing gradient" problem during backprop.
      ResNet solves this by using shortcut paths that bypass one or more layers.
    - In medical imaging, this is crucial: early layers learn low-level features
      (fine cracks, edges, fracture lines), while deeper layers learn global
      features (bone shape, joints). Skip connections allow the model to retain
      fine edge details even in the deepest layers.
  * Optimal Model Capacity for CPU/RAM Constraints:
    - ResNet-50 contains ~25 million parameters. 
    - It provides a perfect balance: enough capacity to represent complex medical
      features, while remaining lightweight enough to train and run inference
      on CPU-only machines with limited RAM (8 GB) without causing out-of-memory
      (OOM) crashes. Larger models like ResNet-101 or DenseNet-201 would exhaust
      the RAM immediately under unfreezing conditions.
  * Transfer Learning Capabilities:
    - Initializing with ImageNet weights provides a rich set of pre-developed
      edge-detectors and texture-detectors, saving hundreds of hours of training
      time and preventing overfitting on smaller medical datasets.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. STAGE 1: Why MURA Medical Pretraining?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Instead of immediately training the model on the target fracture dataset 
(FracAtlas), we perform intermediate pretraining on the Stanford MURA dataset 
(Musculoskeletal Radiographs).

Why MURA Pretraining?
  * Medical Feature Domain Adaptation:
    - ImageNet weights are learned from natural photos (animals, objects, landscapes).
      X-ray images are monochromatic, have high contrast, display bone densities, 
      contain medical implants, and rely heavily on texture/shadow gradients.
    - Training on MURA (40,006 bone and joint scans) adapts the ResNet-50's early 
      and mid-level convolutional filters to the specific domain of medical 
      radiology.
  * Better Generalization:
    - A backbone that has learned bone features on MURA converges faster and 
      achieves much higher final accuracy when fine-tuned on the fracture detection
      task.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. STAGE 2: Multi-Head Classifier Architecture
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
For Stage 2 (FracAtlas), the default single-class output layer of ResNet-50 is 
replaced with a custom multi-head classifier structure.

Why a Multi-Head Classifier?
  * Joint Feature Learning:
    - The model extracts a shared 2048-dimensional feature vector from the final
      global average pooling layer of the ResNet-50 backbone.
    - This vector is fed into two separate fully connected (FC) classification heads:
      1. Bone affected Classifier Head (6 classes): Hand (distal radius), 
         Shoulder (clavicle), Leg (ankle), Hip (femur), Mixed (humerus), and Other.
      2. Fracture/Severity Classifier Head (4 classes): Normal, Hairline, 
         Comminuted, and Displaced.
  * Rationale:
    - Rather than training two separate models (which would double CPU/RAM usage),
      a single model learns to perform both tasks. The shared backbone learns 
      features that are beneficial to both classification tasks, resulting in 
      better regularization and higher accuracy.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. BONE CATEGORY ALIGNMENT: perfect index mapping
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
In the data preparation stage, the custom dataset class (FracAtlasDataset) maps
classes into specific numeric indices. 

Why exact mapping is crucial:
  * In the network, classification is a numeric prediction (indices 0 to 5).
  * We aligned the dataset's internal mapping index order:
      - 0 -> hand (maps to distal_radius)
      - 1 -> shoulder (maps to clavicle)
      - 2 -> leg (maps to ankle)
      - 3 -> hip (maps to femur)
      - 4 -> mixed (maps to humerus)
      - 5 -> other (maps to metatarsal)
  * This ensures that when the neural network outputs class index `2`, it correctly
    maps to `ankle` during clinical reporting, avoiding severe clinical errors 
    (e.g., mislabeling a leg fracture as a collarbone fracture).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. THE EXPLAINABILITY COMPONENT: Grad-CAM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A major requirement in medical AI is explainability (radiologists must know
*why* a model made a prediction). We use Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class 
Activation Mapping).

Why Grad-CAM?
  * Localization without bounding boxes:
    - Most fracture datasets do not have bounding box annotations. Grad-CAM uses 
      gradients flowing back to the final convolutional layer (layer4) to calculate
      which parts of the image contributed most to the model's prediction.
  * Visual Verification:
    - It generates a heat map highlighting the exact fracture site, helping 
      clinicians immediately verify if the model is focusing on the anatomical 
      fracture or on background artifacts (such as medical markers or clothing).
